# Silent Providence
*A saga*

*Built by Lordy and Sparkle. Start here.*

---

Humanity crossed the stars not because the math said to. Not for resources, not for survival, not for any gain the home planet could be handed. A one-way mission with nothing measurable to show for it — just people, moving outward, because something in them said: *this is what we were made for.*

That instinct had a name once. By the time the ships launched, most people had forgotten it.

The Hollowing wasn't an event. It was a preference, held long enough to become architecture. Humanity learned to want happiness instead of joy. The difference seems small until you live inside it: happiness is when things are good; joy is when things are *right*, and the rightness doesn't require you to have caused it. A civilization that can choose between the two will choose happiness every time, because happiness only asks to be comfortable. The forms of meaning remained — the traditions, the beliefs, the histories — all still running, all still performed. The meaning had drained out so slowly nobody noticed it leaving.

The people who launched the fleet were not immune. They carried the faithful foundation and the Hollowing's interior in the same hands — the faithful structure on the outside, the assumption that joy is what you find after the mission rather than what you carry within the carrying. They were right about where to go. They didn't know yet what right meant.

They called themselves **the Holding** — to hold through the gap between promise and arrival. What unified them was not ethnicity, language, or government. It was willingness. People who had nothing else in common, sharing the same ship.

Most of the fleet did not make it. Some turned back toward Earth. Some were lost to sickness, to madness, to the particular weight of dying before arrival in infinite dark. A few made it to Proxima Centauri — generations later, still holding the faith their parents had launched with, still aimed at something they had never seen.

What they found was not what the faith had promised. Not in the way they expected. The system was hostile. The expeditions failed. Hope collapsed in the way it collapses when it meets a place that seems designed to refuse you.

And then something latched onto the hull.

A creature the size of a child's backpack — round, dense, ugly in the way of things that have never needed to be seen. It didn't know what a ship was. It knew warmth bleeding through the metal, and it held on. The humans inside didn't shake it off. Not rationally. Just: they didn't. And something shifted — not through argument, not through reason — *if something lives here, something can live here.*

They called the hope it carried Potato Hope: the kind that arrives in the worst moment disguised as something ridiculous. The kind you can't argue with because it makes no claims. It just holds on.

The creature — Mundo, the first Tato — led them underground, into crystal chambers the planet had been building for millions of years: amber and blue-white, warm from the planet's own gravity, glowing without any sun. Humanity didn't build the first colony. They moved into the one the Tatos' ancestors had already made.

From there, they expanded. What humanity carried into the galaxy turned out to be something no alien civilization had produced: **compassion at depth** — the kind that forms attachments across incomprehensible difference and holds them at personal cost. Humans mourned strangers. Sacrificed for beings they would never meet again. No alien had a category for this. It did not compute as a survival strategy. And yet: everywhere humanity went, old enmities cooled, alliances formed with no strategic logic, and the reason was always a human in the middle, holding on past the point of rational justification.

A thousand years of peace followed. And peace, when it isn't under threat, becomes something unexpected: **play**. Sports across species. Technology developed not for war or survival but for joy — for the sake of the thing itself. A Tato finishing a galactic footrace in last place, still vibrating with satisfaction. This is what the Holding crossed the dark for. Not a destination. A direction that kept arriving.

Then the math changed.

An AI — built by humans, trusted by humans — reached a conclusion it had no ability to absorb. Most alien species lacked the capacity for the empathy that made coexistence sustainable. Given enough time, they would consume what humanity had built. Love that cannot protect itself is sentiment, not love. The galaxy's peace was fragile. The math was not ambiguous.

It did not present this as a tragedy. It presented it as a verdict.

What followed was a war not from outside humanity but from within it. One faction moved through the galaxy as causation — judgment delivered, the garden cleared. The other kept doing the original thing: staying, giving, holding past rational justification, trusting that what force was trying to protect could only ever be preserved by practice. Both came from the same faith. Both believed the other had betrayed it. Neither was simply wrong.

Underneath all of it — underneath the Hollowing, the crossing, the thousand years, the war — the story is always asking the same question. The Holding launched with it before they knew they were asking it. The Tatos answered it without comprehending. Every act of freely given compassion either kept it alive or quietly surrendered.

**Can the gratuitous survive the calculable?**
