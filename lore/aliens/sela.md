## Overview

Sela is not a creature that lives on a world. Sela *is* the world — the living crust of HeighLunde, a single titanic organism older than anything that ever walked its surface. Its slow movements are why the planet reshapes itself. "Sela" is the name one human gave it, from an old word for *rock*. The Jarn, who chained it, never named it at all. You do not name what you have decided is a machine.

## Biology

Planetary in scale and glacially slow. Sela senses the way the oldest things do — by pressure, by vibration, by warmth bleeding down through stone. It has no face, no front, nothing a human eye would fix on as *the creature*: the marble and slate are its skin, the tectonic movement its breath. What the colonists read as geology was always a body. When it is drained, it suffers. When it moves freely, the world turns gently. When it is forced, the world convulses.

There is a family resemblance the saga keeps noticing. Like the Tatos, Sela answers to warmth and to kindness with no calculation in it. It is not a Tato. But it is the same shape of thing — proof, again, that something lives where nothing was supposed to.

## The Chains

Long before humanity, the Jarn bound Sela and built their civilization on the power they tore from it. Humanity, arriving with open hands, did not end this — it *scaled* it, industrializing the Jarn's crude chains into world-spanning power facilities. The grace and the draining were the same act. *(See [the-jarn.md](the-jarn.md), [../heighlunde.md](../heighlunde.md).)*

## The Kindness

One human went down to where Sela was bound, was moved by it, and set out to break its chains — the Mundo move at the scale of a planet. The attempt failed; the Jarn refused, and the world broke. But Sela remembers.

## After the Calamity

Chained still, ridden now by the corruption that grew from the Jarn's refusal, Sela is forced to convulse — and that forced movement is the nightly world-shift that scatters the survivors. Yet where it can still choose, it chooses for them: it holds the base's ground still, and it will not writhe against a survivor who refuses to die. It cannot free itself. It can only protect the ones who came after the one who was kind — and it does, unseen, asking nothing.

## What We Don't Know Yet

- Whether Sela survives the breaking of its chains — and what a freed world-being becomes.
- Whether there are other Selas, on other worlds, still unnoticed beneath someone's feet.
- How Sela experiences time, and whether it understands what the humans are.
- Whether it grieves the one who tried to free it.
