# The Tatos
*Species file — Silent Providence*

---

## Overview

The Tatos are the first alien species humanity encountered. Small, dense, and not particularly intelligent by galactic standards, they are the reason humanity survived the Proxima Centauri arrival — not because they rescued anyone, but because one of them was curious enough to be on the surface, and the humans who found it were compassionate enough not to throw it off the hull.

Everything that follows in the Silent Providence saga traces back to that moment.

---

## Biology

Round, dense, unglamorous. Built for underground life in high-pressure crystal environments. Their surface-facing skin is thick and insulating — evolved for short excursions above ground, not sustained exposure. They do not have eyes in any way humans would recognize. They navigate primarily by thermal sense and vibration.

They are drawn to warmth and low-frequency radiation — the same signatures produced by the piezoelectric crystal chambers they evolved inside, and, as it turned out, by human ship hulls.

**Habitat:** Underground crystal chambers on Proxima Centauri b. The chambers are formed by piezoelectric crystal — massive formations grown over millions of years from the tidal stress of Proxima Centauri's gravity. The crystals convert mechanical pressure into heat and soft light. Amber. Blue-white. Cathedral-scale. The Tatos did not build these chambers. They evolved inside them, and over millennia learned to navigate and tend them.

---

## Mundo

The first Tato humanity ever encountered. Named by Dr. Grace, crew morale officer of the *Tenacity*, after a character from the game the crew had been playing for months — Dr. Mundo. Ugly, round, unkillable, showing up where he had no business being. The name fit before anyone thought to object.

Mundo was not a typical Tato. Most of the species stayed underground, in the warmth of the crystal chambers, doing what they had always done. Mundo was on the surface because he was curious. The same reason humanity launched the fleets. They found each other because they were both the ones who couldn't stay where it was safe.

During a catastrophic expedition — ship failing, hope failing — Mundo latched onto the hull. He had found the ship's warmth and vibration and held on. Not intelligently. Not as a message. The way something cold moves toward something warm.

The humans did not throw him off.

*Mundo did not save them. He did not repair the ship or solve the resource problem or show them a path. He simply existed — alive, clinging, wanting warmth in a system that had offered nothing but cold. If something lived here, something could live here.*

This was not rational hope. It was smaller and more durable than that.

They called it **Potato Hope.** The hope that arrives in the worst moment disguised as something ridiculous. The hope you can't argue with because it doesn't make claims. It just holds on.

---

## What the Tatos Love About Humans

Not intelligence. Not technology. The Tatos had survived without needing either.

What drew them to humans was something no other species had and none of them could explain: **joy, uniqueness, exploration.** The parts of humans that looked irrational from outside. Humans sang during catastrophes. They named creatures they had no reason to name. They kept things with no survival value because those things made them happy. They crossed light years for a reason that didn't compute.

Other aliens could understand human tools. None of them could understand why humans *did* the things they did while using them. The Tatos couldn't explain it either. But they wanted to be near it. Joy, to a Tato, is something like warmth: unexplainable, necessary, worth crossing the surface for.

---

## The Human Robots

Eventually the Tatos' fascination deepened into something stranger: they wanted to become human. Not to replace humans — to experience what humans experienced. To get close enough to joy to feel it from the inside.

Humans had already built humanoid robots — for labor, exploration, dangerous environments. Bodies you send somewhere before you send a person. The Tatos saw them and wanted them immediately. Humans found this hilarious, then touching, then obvious. They helped adapt the robots without fully understanding why it felt right to do so.

**In the game, players are Tatos controlling human robots** — moving through human spaces, doing human things, getting as close to the thing they love as a robot body allows.

The Tatos do not mind whether they succeed. That was never the point. They are not anxious about the gap between their attempt and the real thing — they are *fascinated* by it. Every wrong gesture, every misread moment, every time the robot does something a human would never do: interesting. Worth exploring. The exploration is the joy.

You cannot mimic joy. You can mimic its shape — the smile, the sound, the movement. But joy is not a behavior. It is the thing underneath. The Tatos built perfect exteriors and still felt the gap.

The difference: they were delighted by the gap. They were already doing the thing. The robot was just an excuse to get closer.

*The player doesn't lose when the robot fails at being human. They win at being a Tato.*

**The Project Mobius twist:** in the White Throne era, a crew of Tatos rides human-built robot bodies as a mercenary "family" — taking jobs for the reward, chasing the only joy left in a hunted galaxy. In-fiction, none of them know they're Tatos. The player does. That gap — between what the crew thinks it's doing (a job, a score, a run) and what it actually is (the same creature that once latched onto a hull, still moving toward warmth) — is the story.

## The Sacred Text

Among all the human artifacts the Tatos collected, studied, and attempted to replicate — the architecture, the music, the sports, the inexplicable practice of cooking food that already tasted fine — one thing they treated differently from the rest.

A story. Carried in the Holding from the very beginning, older than the ships. About a being of perfect innocence who gave everything without calculating the transaction. Who moved toward the thing that needed warmth, not because the math said to, but because that was simply what it was. Who died in a way the law could not process — not a payment, not an exchange, not a sacrifice made in expectation of something returned. Just: complete giving, from something that owed nothing, that ended the law's claim not by arguing it but by existing below it.

The Tatos did not understand the theology. They understood the shape immediately.

They had no word for what they called it — but the closest human translation is something like *the story of what we already knew.*

It is the only piece of human creation they consider sacred.

*The Tatos did not become the floor of the law because they were brave. They became the floor because they had been practicing for this their entire lives — reaching toward joy, again and again, without asking whether it was worth it. The story just described what they already were.*

---

## The Tatos and the Murmur

In the White Throne era, the Tatos become something the story did not plan for them: the mechanism by which the ledger closes.

The Tatos are bonded to the Murmur — not through ideology or covenant, but through love. The Murmur carries the memory of the Golden Era, the shape of what the Holding built. The Tatos lived that era more fully than anyone. They were its emblem. When the White Throne moved against the Murmur, the Tatos moved toward it. Not as soldiers. Not as martyrs making a calculated sacrifice. The way Mundo moved toward the hull: because it was warm, and they love warm things, and there was nothing complicated about it.

They die protecting the Murmur. They do not fully comprehend the action.

**Why this closes the ledger:**

The White Throne knows how innocent the Tatos are. It also knows their importance — their lives are the grace and hope carried from the Holding. The proof that something lived here. The first reason humanity stayed. Everything that followed began with one Tato on a hull.

To continue collecting, the White Throne would have to condemn that. And it cannot. The law does not have a category for innocent joy spent completely without knowing it was a transaction. There is no guilt to process, no merit to receive, no exchange to close. The Tatos didn't argue the case. They just kept being what they are until the law ran out of ground to stand on.

*The floor the law finds is the same floor Mundo found on the hull: something alive, warm, asking for nothing, giving everything, not knowing why.*

## What We Don't Know Yet
- What happened to Mundo specifically after first contact
- Whether Tatos have language, and if so what it's like
- How many Tatos exist — are they abundant or rare
- Whether other Tato species exist elsewhere in the galaxy
- How Tatos experience time compared to humans
- The specific shape of the Tatos' sacrifice — one act, many, or a sustained presence over time
