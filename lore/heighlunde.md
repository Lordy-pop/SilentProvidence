# HeighLunde
*Silent Providence — an Early Expansion chapter*
*The planet of the Driftbound campaign. Where the law arrived with the grace, and the grace was refused.*

---

## Placement

HeighLunde belongs to the **Early Expansion** era — after Proxima, when humanity was spreading outward and meeting new species for the first time. It is a single planet, and a single complete telling of the saga's oldest movement: humanity arrives carrying compassion; the compassion uplifts; and with the knowledge of the good comes the weight of the law. Most of the saga watches that weight get *calculated*. HeighLunde is where it became *physical*.

## The World That Was

White plains of marble. Long ridges of dark slate. A world under a permanent night, beautiful and severe. The humans who colonized it named it HeighLunde.

What the colonists did not know — what almost no one knew — was that the ground was alive. The crust of HeighLunde is a single titanic being, **Sela**, older than anything that ever walked its surface. Its slow movements are why the world reshapes itself. *(Full file: [aliens/sela.md](aliens/sela.md).)*

## The Jarn

The world already had masters: the **Jarn**, an iron-proud people who, ages before humanity, had bound Sela in chains and built their civilization on the power they wrung from its suffering. To the Jarn this was not cruelty. It was inheritance — the throne passed down, the ground beneath it a thing to be used. *(Full file: [aliens/the-jarn.md](aliens/the-jarn.md).)*

## The Uplift

Humanity did to the Jarn what it did everywhere it went: it stayed, it shared, it was kind past the point of strategy. Under human help the Jarn rose — and "rising" here meant **harnessing the planet's resources**, which on HeighLunde meant one thing only: harnessing Sela. Human engineering took the Jarn's crude chains and industrialized them into power facilities that ringed the world. The grace and the draining were the same act. The prosperity was real. So was the scream underneath it.

This is the saga's most uncomfortable truth, told plainly on one planet: **the grace and the harm were not separable.** The compassion that lifted the Jarn was the same hand that scaled their oldest sin.

## The Kindness, and the Refusal

Then humanity learned what it was standing on. One human went down to Sela, and was moved by it the way the *Tenacity* crew was moved by a creature on a hull — not because the math said to, but because that was simply what they were. And they moved to free it.

The Jarn refused.

To them, the humans' mercy was the invasion's final form: the stranger declaring their oldest law a crime, reaching to tear out the foundation of the world and calling the theft *kindness*. **"Your grace is an invasion."** They would not trade their chains for the strangers' ones. They clenched their grip.

And neither side was simply lying. The grace was real. The grievance was real. The care, the conquest, the cost — all true at once. HeighLunde does not resolve it. It only shows the outcome.

## The Calamity

When the Jarn refused, the facilities broke. Radiation flooded HeighLunde — but the radiation was never the danger. It woke the world's moss and bacteria and twisted them into a corruption that spread without end: red and purple drowning the marble, biology remade into things that should not be.

The corruption is not mindless. It is the Jarn's refusal made flesh — their collective will to keep the throne, crystallized into a living, growing thing. **The corruption and the Jarn are one.** Its sovereign and its mind is the last emperor, **Valdar**.

This is the endogenous answer the saga left open: no White Throne came to judge HeighLunde. The Jarn judged themselves. The ledger came due not as a verdict delivered, but as a sickness grown from the refusal itself.

## The Run

In the grinding aftermath, the human colonists and their descendants survive in an anchored base, fighting toward the only ending: the death of Emperor Valdar, which is the death of the corruption, which is the breaking of Sela's chains.

They do not know that the chained god is on their side. Sela remembers the one who was kind, and shields the ones who came after, unseen — anchoring their base, sparing the ground beneath the survivor who refuses to die. *(The mechanics, told from the survivors' side, live in the Driftbound design docs.)*

## What HeighLunde Gives the Saga

- **The endogenous threat.** A third answer to *"does the cosmic threat arrive, or did the war cause it?"* — neither. Refused grace becomes biological.
- **The grace-refused parable.** What a species that received compassion and would not extend it actually looks like.
- **The physically superior species** — Sela.
- **A root of the Solitudes** — the Jarn's "your grace is an invasion" is the faction's oldest ancestor.

## Open Questions

- The Jarn's own name for Sela, and for HeighLunde.
- The name and fate of the human who showed Sela compassion.
- Did any Jarn break with the refusal — a dissenter, the first of a different line?
- How does HeighLunde's story reach the wider saga — is it remembered, buried, or rediscovered in the White Throne era?
- Does Sela survive the breaking of its chains — and what does a freed world-being become?
